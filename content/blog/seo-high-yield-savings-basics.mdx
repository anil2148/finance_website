A 0.15% APY difference looks big in ads and small in real life. For emergency and short-term cash, transfer speed and account reliability can matter more than squeezing every basis point.

## Scorecard: grade each account out of 100

- **Yield durability (25 points):** Is APY stable or frequently promotional?
- **Access speed (30 points):** How quickly can you move money to your spending account?
- **Rules and limits (20 points):** Minimums, tiers, inactivity triggers, withdrawal constraints.
- **Service reliability (15 points):** app uptime, support responsiveness, dispute handling.
- **Cash management fit (10 points):** alerts, buckets, linked account controls.

Any account under 70/100 is usually a poor fit for core reserves.

## Quick math example

$20,000 balance:
- Account X at 4.60% APY vs Account Y at 4.45% APY
- Annual interest difference ≈ $30 before tax

If Account X routinely delays transfers by one extra business day during urgent needs, that convenience gap can cost more than the APY gain.

## Decision framework by money type

- **Emergency reserve:** prioritize access speed + reliability.
- **Known short-term goal (6–24 months):** prioritize yield and organization tools.
- **Daily spending buffer:** keep a portion in checking for immediate liquidity.

## Questions to ask before opening

- How long do inbound and outbound transfers actually take in normal conditions?
- Is customer support reachable by phone and chat during work hours?
- Does the account have quirks that could freeze or delay access?

Document answers before moving emergency cash.

## Portfolio setup suggestion

Many households use a two-account cash stack:

1. **Operating buffer in checking** for immediate bills.
2. **Core reserve in HYSA** for short-term safety and yield.

This setup reduces forced transfers during urgent purchases.
