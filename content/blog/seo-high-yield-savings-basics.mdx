A high APY gets attention, but account behavior determines whether a savings account is actually useful when life gets messy.

## The 5-factor scorecard that matters

Use this weighted score instead of rate-chasing:

1. **APY (30%)** — competitive yield after promo periods
2. **Access speed (25%)** — external transfer speed and cutoff times
3. **Friction/rules (20%)** — withdrawal limits, minimum balance rules
4. **Safety/structure (15%)** — FDIC/NCUA coverage and institution clarity
5. **User reliability (10%)** — support quality, app stability, alerts

Two accounts can both offer 4.60% APY, but one can take 3 business days to transfer while the other transfers next day. That difference matters when an emergency bill is due Friday.

## Example comparison (real-world decision style)

Assume $20,000 emergency savings.

- Account A: 4.70% APY, slow transfers, weak app support
- Account B: 4.45% APY, faster transfers, better controls

Estimated annual interest gap is about $50 ($20,000 × 0.25%). Many households should take Account B if reliability prevents overdrafts, late fees, or stress borrowing.

## Who should choose which type?

- **Emergency-fund saver:** prioritize fast external transfer and clean mobile controls.
- **Sinking-fund planner:** prioritize sub-accounts/buckets for taxes, travel, insurance.
- **Rate optimizer with stable cash:** prioritize APY and acceptable transfer lag.

## What to avoid

- Moving money every 2 weeks for tiny APY differences
- Ignoring transfer hold periods and withdrawal frictions
- Confusing money market funds with insured savings deposits
- Storing all cash in one institution above insurance limits

## What should you do now?

### Best option if you are a beginner

Choose one account in the top range, automate deposits, and stop shopping for 6 months.

### Best option if your priority is low effort

Keep one checking + one HYSA at institutions that connect cleanly. Fewer transfers usually means fewer mistakes.

### Best option if your priority is maximizing yield

Use a two-tier setup: operating cash in primary HYSA, longer-horizon cash in CDs/T-bills only after emergency money is complete.
