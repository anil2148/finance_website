Most people shop HYSAs backward: they sort by APY and ignore account behavior. That is how you end up with “great yield” but slow transfer rails, confusing limits, and support queues when cash is urgent.

Below is a field workflow you can run in one evening before moving emergency reserves.

## Start with a 3-lane cash map

Separate cash by **job**, then match each job to the right account lane:

- **Lane 1: Same-day liquidity** (rent gaps, car repairs this week)
- **Lane 2: Emergency reserve core** (job-loss buffer)
- **Lane 3: Planned savings** (vacation, tax bill, annual insurance)

Decision rule: only Lane 2 and Lane 3 are HYSA candidates. Lane 1 stays where debit access is immediate.

## The operations scorecard (100 points)

Use this weighting before you compare rates:

| Factor | Weight | What to verify |
|---|---:|---|
| Transfer reliability and speed | 30 | Real outbound transfer timing to your checking account |
| Yield durability | 25 | Promo vs non-promo APY behavior over time |
| Rules and friction | 20 | Hold times, transfer limits, minimums, closure rules |
| Support execution | 15 | Time to resolve locked-account or transfer issues |
| Controls and alerts | 10 | Balance alerts, buckets, naming, automation |

Anything below **70/100** should not hold core emergency money.

## Worked comparison: why lower APY can still win

Scenario: you keep $28,000 in reserve.

- **Account A:** 4.60% APY, but outbound transfers often land in 2–3 business days.
- **Account B:** 4.42% APY, same-day to next-day transfer reliability, better in-app controls.

Rough annual gross-yield gap is modest, but a single delayed transfer during a cash emergency can cost far more than the APY difference. For reserve cash, reliability is usually the higher-value choice.

## 14-day test before moving full balances

Do this before consolidating money:

1. Link checking and push a small inbound transfer.
2. Run one outbound transfer on a weekday morning.
3. Run a second outbound transfer before a weekend.
4. Record actual settlement times and support response quality.
5. Confirm all limits and hold policies in writing.

If the account fails speed or clarity tests, reject it even with a top APY headline.

## Common mistakes that create avoidable risk

- Moving 100% of emergency cash to one new account without live transfer testing.
- Assuming “FDIC insured” means operations will be fast during high-volume periods.
- Ignoring transfer cutoffs until a deadline week.
