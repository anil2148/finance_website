## Who this is for

This guide is for new investors who are worried about “doing it wrong” and want to avoid the most common early mistakes.
If you’ve been hesitant to start because you’re afraid of choosing poorly, this will help you focus on a small number of things that matter most.

## Why beginner investing mistakes matter

Early mistakes can have long-lasting effects, especially if they cause you to stop investing or stay in cash for years.
The goal isn’t to be perfect—it’s to avoid the most avoidable errors so you can keep moving forward.

## How mistakes show up in real life

Beginner mistakes often look like:

- Opening multiple accounts without a clear plan for each one.
- Jumping in and out of the market based on headlines or social media.
- Picking individual stocks without understanding how concentrated the portfolio has become.
- Ignoring fees and account costs that quietly reduce returns over time.

Recognizing these patterns is the first step to building a more deliberate approach.

## Common beginner investing mistakes

Below are some of the most frequent issues new investors run into and what to consider instead.

### Waiting too long to get started

Many people delay investing for years while they “wait to have more money” or try to learn everything first.
Starting small with an amount you can afford and gradually scaling up can be more effective than waiting for a perfect moment.

### Chasing recent performance

It’s tempting to buy whatever has recently gone up the most.
Instead, focus on a plan tied to your goals and risk tolerance, not last year’s winners.

### Over-concentrating in a single idea

Putting too much into a single stock, sector, or theme can make your portfolio overly dependent on one outcome.
Using diversified funds or ETFs can spread risk across many companies and regions.

### Ignoring fees and costs

Account fees, fund expense ratios, and trading costs all add up over time.
When comparing options, consider both what you gain and what it costs to use the service.

## What to do instead

To reduce the impact of these mistakes:

- Define your main goal for each account (retirement, medium-term savings, etc.).
- Choose a simple, diversified mix you can explain in your own words.
- Automate contributions and set calendar reminders for periodic check-ins.
- Keep a short list of rules about when you will change your plan and when you won’t.

## What to do next

You don’t need to avoid every minor misstep to make progress as a new investor.
The key is to avoid the most damaging habits and build a routine that keeps you investing over time.

- Use an investment or compound interest calculator to see how regular contributions could grow.
