## Who this is for
Borrowers choosing between payment certainty and potentially lower initial rates on personal loans, HELOCs, or adjustable products.

## Core difference
- **Fixed rate**: rate and payment structure stay stable through the term.
- **Variable rate**: starts at a quoted rate, then adjusts with a benchmark (often prime rate or SOFR-linked structures).

## Why this matters now
Rate direction in 2026 is uncertain. If benchmarks move down, variable borrowers may benefit. If they move up or stay high, payment risk remains.

## When fixed usually makes sense
- You need predictable monthly cash flow.
- You expect to hold debt for 5+ years.
- Your budget has little room for payment shocks.
- You want a clear payoff plan for consolidation.

## When variable can make sense
- You expect to repay in under 3 years.
- Initial variable discount is substantial vs fixed.
- You can tolerate moderate payment increases.
- You understand cap structure and adjustment terms.

## Key tradeoffs
Fixed rates often cost more upfront but buy certainty. Variable rates may start lower but can become more expensive if benchmarks rise.

## Example scenario
A borrower choosing fixed at 10.9% vs variable at 8.4% should model at least three paths:
1. Benchmark unchanged
2. Benchmark rises by 2 points
3. Benchmark falls by 1 point

Without that model, the lower starting payment can be misleading.

## Common mistakes
- Selecting variable only for the lowest first payment
- Ignoring periodic and lifetime caps
- Choosing fixed without quantifying the “stability premium”

## Next steps
1. Ask for full adjustment terms (index, margin, caps, reset frequency).
2. Run a stress test with +2% and +3% scenarios.
3. Compare total cost under each path.
4. Confirm prepayment rules before signing.
