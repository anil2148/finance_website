The 50/30/20 rule is a planning baseline, not a law. Real households deal with uneven income, high rent, and irregular expenses that require adaptation.

## Start with take-home pay

Always apply the split to after-tax income, then classify:
- **Needs:** housing, utilities, groceries, insurance, debt minimums
- **Wants:** discretionary lifestyle spending
- **Goals:** savings, extra debt payoff, investing

## Example with a realistic adaptation

Take-home pay: **$5,400/month**
- Needs: **$3,100** (57%)
- Wants: **$950** (18%)
- Goals: **$1,350** (25%)

This is still strong if elevated needs are temporary and goals remain funded.

## Adaptation playbook by situation

### High housing-cost city
Trim wants first, renegotiate recurring bills quarterly, and build sinking funds for annual expenses.

### Debt-heavy phase
Use a temporary **60/15/25** or **65/10/25** split while paying down high-interest balances.

### Variable income
Apply percentage rules to each paycheck and automate transfers immediately when income lands.

## Mistakes that quietly break the budget

- Treating annual expenses like surprises instead of planned sinking funds.
- Using one static budget template for every month.
- Leaving savings manual instead of automated.
