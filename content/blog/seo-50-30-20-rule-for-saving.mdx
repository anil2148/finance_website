The 50/30/20 rule works best as a planning baseline, not a strict law. Real households face rent pressure, income volatility, and debt burdens that require tactical adjustments.

## Start with your after-tax income
Use take-home pay, not gross income. Then group spending into:
- **Needs** (housing, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt)
- **Wants** (dining out, travel, optional subscriptions)
- **Goals** (savings, extra debt payoff, investing)

## Example with a realistic adaptation
Take-home pay: **$5,200/month**
- Needs: **$2,900** (56%)
- Wants: **$1,000** (19%)
- Goals: **$1,300** (25%)

This can still be a strong plan if needs are temporarily elevated and goal contributions stay intentional.

## Adjustment playbook by situation
### High-rent market
Cap wants first, then renegotiate recurring bills every 90 days.

### Debt-heavy phase
Move to a temporary **60/15/25** or **65/10/25** split until high-interest balances fall.

### Variable income
Apply percentages to each paycheck and route transfers automatically.

## Mistakes that break the system
- Treating annual/irregular costs as “surprises” instead of sinking funds
- Using one generic budget for every month
- Leaving savings manual (automation usually determines success)
