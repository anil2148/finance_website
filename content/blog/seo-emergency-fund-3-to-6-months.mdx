“Three to six months” is a decent starting slogan, but it is too generic for real households. Your target should be based on replacement risk, not round numbers.

## Step 1: Calculate true essentials
Include housing, utilities, food, insurance, transportation, minimum debt payments, and critical child/elder care. Exclude optional subscriptions and discretionary spending.

If essentials are **$3,400/month**, that is your baseline.

## Step 2: Pick a risk multiplier
- **3–4 months:** dual stable income, strong job security
- **4–5 months:** single income or moderate commission volatility
- **6+ months:** self-employed, cyclical income, or high dependent risk

With $3,400 essentials and a 5-month multiplier, your target is **$17,000**.

## Step 3: Build in stages (so you do not burn out)
1. **Quick buffer:** $1,000 to prevent small shocks from becoming debt
2. **Stability layer:** 1 full month of essentials
3. **Full reserve:** 3–6+ month risk-adjusted target

## Where to hold the money
For most households, this should be in a high-liquidity account, not in volatile assets. Yield matters, but reliable access matters more during emergencies.

## Common mistakes
- Investing emergency cash in risky assets during bull markets
- Setting an aggressive target without automating contributions
- Treating every expense as an “emergency” and never replenishing
