“Save three to six months” sounds useful, but most households still do not know what number to fund first. The better approach is to size your reserve from **re-employment risk + household dependency + cash-flow rigidity**.

## Quick decision score (5-minute setup)

Add points for each condition that applies now:

- Variable or commission-heavy income: **+2**
- Single-income household: **+2**
- Dependents with fixed monthly commitments: **+1**
- Industry with visible layoff or demand cycles: **+2**
- High unavoidable medical/transport risk: **+1**

### Pick your reserve band

- **0–2 points:** 3 months of essentials
- **3–5 points:** 4–5 months of essentials
- **6–8 points:** 6–8 months of essentials

## Worked case: one earner, cyclical industry

Essentials baseline: **$4,100/month**.

Risk points:
- one earner (+2)
- variable pay (+2)
- two dependents (+1)
- cyclical industry (+2)

Total = **7 points**, so target band = **6–8 months**.

Practical starting target = 6 months = **$24,600**.

## Funding sequence that avoids burnout

Do not aim for $24,600 on day one. Fund in stages:

1. **Stage 1 (stability floor):** first $2,000 for urgent shocks.
2. **Stage 2 (cash-flow protection):** one full essentials month ($4,100).
3. **Stage 3 (full reserve):** complete remaining target with automations and variable-income sweeps.

Operating rule for irregular income: route **50% of bonuses/refunds/side-income** into reserve until Stage 3 is complete.

## Account choice matters as much as target size

Emergency funds need immediate access and low operational friction.

- Keep at least one month in the fastest-access account lane.
- If your transfer times are slow, hold a small checking buffer for same-day needs.
- Prefer predictable account behavior over chasing tiny APY differences.
