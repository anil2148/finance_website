"Save 3 to 6 months" is a useful rule of thumb, but not a complete plan. The right target depends on income stability, household obligations, and replacement timeline risk.

## Step 1: Calculate true essential monthly spend

Include only expenses you must keep paying in disruption periods:
- Housing
- Utilities
- Groceries
- Insurance
- Transportation
- Minimum debt payments
- Critical care obligations

If essentials are **$3,600/month**, that is your reserve baseline.

## Step 2: Choose a risk multiplier

- **3–4 months:** dual stable incomes, low volatility employment
- **4–5 months:** single income or moderate earnings volatility
- **6+ months:** self-employed, cyclical income, or high dependent obligations

At $3,600 essentials with a 5-month target, reserve goal = **$18,000**.

## Step 3: Build in milestones

1. **Shock buffer:** first $1,000
2. **Stability layer:** one full month of essentials
3. **Full reserve:** risk-adjusted 3–6+ months

Milestones reduce burnout and make progress measurable.

## Where to keep emergency money

Prioritize liquidity and reliability first. For most households, that means a strong savings account, not volatile assets.

## Common mistakes

- Investing emergency reserves during bull markets.
- Setting a high target without automation.
- Treating predictable annual bills as emergencies.
