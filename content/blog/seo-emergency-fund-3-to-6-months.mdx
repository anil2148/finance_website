Most people hear “save 3 to 6 months” and freeze because that range is too wide to use. A better approach is to model **how long your household needs to recover after an income shock**.

## Step 1: calculate survival burn (not total spending)

List only expenses that keep your household stable:

- housing + utilities
- groceries + basic household supplies
- insurance + prescriptions
- minimum debt payments
- transport required for work and family obligations

Ignore optional spending in this stage.

Example household:

- housing/utilities: $2,150
- groceries: $780
- insurance/health: $520
- debt minimums: $450
- transport/phone: $430

**Survival burn = $4,330/month**.

## Step 2: estimate realistic re-employment window

Choose one:

- **Fast market, portable role:** 2–3 months
- **Specialized role, moderate openings:** 4–5 months
- **Cyclical industry / regional constraints:** 6–8 months

If your household depends on one earner or has children, add one month of margin.

## Step 3: convert timeline into a reserve target

Formula:

`Survival burn × recovery months + one-shock buffer`

For this household in a 5-month window:

- $4,330 × 5 = $21,650
- one-shock buffer (car/medical/home): $2,500
- **Target reserve: $24,150**

This is far more actionable than a generic “maybe six months.”

## Funding architecture that survives real life

Use three buckets with different jobs:

1. **Shock buffer ($2k–$3k):** immediate surprises
2. **Bridge month:** one full month of survival burn
3. **Core reserve:** remainder toward target

Automate with two transfer rules:

- fixed transfer every payday
- variable transfer of 50% of bonus/tax refund/freelance deposits

## Decision checkpoint: save faster or pay debt first?

Use this rule:

- If debt APR is above ~20%, split excess cash between reserve and payoff.
- If debt APR is moderate and your reserve is below one month, prioritize reserve first.

Reason: one emergency without cash often creates new high-rate debt, which restarts the cycle.
