“Three to six months” is a starting sentence, not a plan. Your target should reflect **replacement speed**, **income volatility**, and **how many people depend on your paycheck**.

## Use this risk score first

Give yourself one point for each statement that is true:

- My income is variable (commission, freelance, seasonal).
- My household has one primary earner.
- My role is in a cyclical industry.
- I support dependents with fixed monthly obligations.
- My health or transportation costs are hard to predict.

**0–1 points:** start near 3 months.
**2–3 points:** target 4–5 months.
**4–5 points:** plan for 6+ months.

## Build in layers, not one giant goal

### Layer 1: Shock buffer
Get to $1,000–$2,000 quickly for minor disruptions.

### Layer 2: One full essentials month
Cover rent/mortgage, food, utilities, insurance, debt minimums.

### Layer 3: Full risk-adjusted reserve
Extend to your selected 3–6+ month target.

This staged approach reduces fatigue and keeps momentum.

## Scenario walkthrough

Essentials spend = $3,900/month.
Risk score = 4 (single income + cyclical work + two dependents + variable bonus).
Recommended target = 6 months.

Reserve goal = $23,400.

Build path:
- First 90 days: reach $3,000.
- Next 6 months: reach one full month ($3,900).
- Long run: automate remaining gap over 18–24 months.

## Funding cadence that survives real life

Use two contribution lanes so progress does not depend on one perfect month:

- **Base lane:** fixed automatic transfer each payday.
- **Variable lane:** percentage of irregular income (bonus, refund, side income).

Even a small base lane keeps the plan alive during tighter months.

## When to pause contributions temporarily

Pause only for defined events: active job loss, urgent medical expense, or required home/transport safety repairs. Resume with the next paycheck cycle once stability returns.
