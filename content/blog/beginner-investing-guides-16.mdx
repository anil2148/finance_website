## Who this is for

This guide is for beginners who want a straightforward way to invest for the long term without picking individual stocks.
If you like the idea of broad diversification but don’t want to manage a large list of investments, a simple 3-fund portfolio can be a good starting point.

## What is a 3-fund portfolio?

A 3-fund portfolio is a diversified mix built from three core building blocks:

- A broad domestic stock fund.
- A broad international stock fund.
- A broad bond fund.

Together, these can give you exposure to thousands of companies and a buffer from bonds, using only a handful of investments.

## Example allocations by investor profile

These example mixes are for illustration only—adjust based on your time horizon and comfort with short-term ups and downs.

### Long-term, higher risk tolerance

- 50–60% domestic stock index fund.
- 20–30% international stock index fund.
- 10–20% bond fund.

This type of mix will move up and down more but has higher long-term growth potential.

### Moderate risk tolerance

- 35–45% domestic stock index fund.
- 15–25% international stock index fund.
- 30–40% bond fund.

This mix may feel more comfortable for investors who want growth but are more sensitive to volatility.

## How to build your 3-fund portfolio

When building your own version, consider:

1. **Account type first.**
   Decide whether you are using a retirement account, a taxable brokerage, or both.

2. **Choose your three funds.**
   Look for diversified index funds or ETFs that clearly describe which part of the market they track.

3. **Set your target percentages.**
   Decide what portion of your portfolio each fund should represent based on your risk tolerance and time horizon.

4. **Automate contributions.**
   Direct new contributions toward your target mix, adjusting periodically if one fund has grown faster than the others.

## Rebalancing and staying on track

Over time, some parts of your portfolio will grow faster than others.
Rebalancing helps keep your mix aligned with the risk level you originally chose.

You can:

- Review your allocation once or twice per year.
- Direct new contributions to the underweight fund instead of trading frequently.
- Make modest adjustments if your goals or time horizon change.

## Common mistakes to avoid

When using a 3-fund portfolio, try to avoid:

- Adding many extra overlapping funds that complicate things without clear benefit.
- Ignoring your target percentages for years, especially after large market moves.
- Comparing your returns too frequently to short-term market headlines.

Keeping the structure simple makes it easier to stay invested through normal ups and downs.

## What to do next

A 3-fund portfolio is one way to get diversified exposure with minimal complexity.
Your next step is to check whether this approach fits your goals and comfort with risk.

- Use an investment or retirement calculator to see how a steady contribution plan into this kind of mix could grow over time.
- Review investment platforms to find one that offers low-cost index funds and tools for easy rebalancing.
