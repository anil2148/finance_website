Preapproval is mostly a file-quality test. Strong applicants still get delayed when records are incomplete, inconsistent, or hard to trace.

## Build your file in three folders

### Folder A: Income proof
- Last 30 days of pay stubs
- Last two years of W-2s (or 1099/tax returns for self-employment)
- Additional records for variable compensation when relevant

### Folder B: Asset proof
- Recent bank statements (all pages)
- Investment account statements if funds are used for down payment or reserves
- Source documentation for any large recent deposits

### Folder C: Liability and identity proof
- Current debt obligations and monthly minimums
- Government identification and address verification

## Timeline expectations when paperwork is clean

- **Day 1:** application + credit pull
- **Days 2–4:** underwriter first review
- **Days 5–9:** follow-up conditions and clarification
- **Days 10+:** decision path if conditions are satisfied

Delays usually come from missing statement pages, undocumented transfers, or new debt opened mid-process.

## Preapproval risk flags (and fixes)

- **Risk:** frequent large account transfers.  
  **Fix:** maintain a clear paper trail and minimize new movements.

- **Risk:** new credit inquiry during review.  
  **Fix:** pause non-essential applications until preapproval is complete.

- **Risk:** screenshots instead of full statements.  
  **Fix:** provide complete official PDFs.

## Borrower behavior rules during review

Until the preapproval process is complete:

- Avoid financing furniture, appliances, or vehicles.
- Keep payroll deposits consistent where possible.
- Respond to lender requests the same day to prevent queue delays.

These habits reduce avoidable re-underwriting loops.

## Offer readiness check

Before using a preapproval letter to make an offer, confirm you still match the file that was approved: debt levels, employment status, and available funds should be materially unchanged.

If your profile changed, request an updated review before making offers. Acting on stale preapproval assumptions can create contract risk if financing terms tighten.
