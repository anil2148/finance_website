Tax efficiency is not a one-time trick. It is a set of repeatable placement and rebalancing choices that preserve more of your return over time.

## Start with one policy page

Write and keep a short policy with three rules:

1. **Account-location rule:** decide which holdings belong in taxable vs tax-advantaged accounts.
2. **Turnover rule:** define what level of strategy change is worth creating taxable events.
3. **Rebalancing rule:** specify how often and where you rebalance first.

This reduces reactive, tax-expensive decisions.

## Placement logic that many investors miss

- Taxable account: typically better for low-turnover, tax-efficient core holdings.
- Tax-advantaged accounts: often better for holdings that generate more tax drag.
- Rebalance in sheltered accounts first when possible, then use taxable accounts if needed.

## Scenario comparison

You have $12,000 to allocate this year and two choices:

- Add to taxable brokerage for flexibility.
- Fill tax-advantaged contribution room first.

The better choice depends on expected holding period, current/future tax bracket assumptions, and withdrawal timing. Model both paths before committing.

## Loss harvesting: useful with guardrails

Harvesting can help only when executed systematically.

Set rules in advance:
- acceptable replacement funds,
- wash-sale window controls,
- documented rationale for each trade.

Without rules, harvesting often becomes expensive overtrading.

## Annual maintenance checklist

Run this once per year, ideally before year-end:

- Review realized gains and losses to avoid surprise tax bills.
- Check whether any funds distributed larger-than-expected taxable income.
- Reconfirm that account locations still match your strategy.
- Update your rebalancing thresholds based on portfolio size.

A yearly review catches drift before it becomes expensive.

## Implementation sequence for beginners

1. Keep contributions automated first.
2. Add basic account-location rules second.
3. Add harvesting and advanced tactics only after steps 1 and 2 are stable.

This sequence prevents “tax optimization theater” where complexity increases but after-tax outcomes do not.
