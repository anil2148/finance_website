The Roth versus Traditional IRA question is not a personality test. It is a tax-rate comparison across two different time periods: now and retirement.

## The simplest decision rule

- Choose **Traditional IRA** when current-year tax deduction value is high and you expect lower retirement tax rates.
- Choose **Roth IRA** when your current tax rate is relatively low and you expect higher taxable income later.

When uncertain, split contributions across both to diversify tax risk.

## What each account optimizes

### Traditional IRA

- Potential current-year deduction (subject to income and workplace-plan rules)
- Tax-deferred growth
- Taxable withdrawals in retirement

### Roth IRA

- Contributions are post-tax
- Qualified withdrawals are tax-free
- Better withdrawal flexibility for contribution basis

## Bracket-based examples

If you are in the 22% bracket and likely to retire in a lower bracket, pre-tax deductions can be valuable now.

If you are early career in the 12% bracket with high future earning potential, paying taxes now for tax-free future withdrawals can be rational.

## Income-limit and eligibility friction

Traditional IRA deduction eligibility phases out based on income and plan participation. Roth IRA direct contribution eligibility also phases out at higher incomes.

For households above Roth direct limits, a backdoor Roth process may be available, but it requires careful handling of pro-rata rules if pre-tax IRA balances already exist.

## Common mistakes

- Making the decision once and never revisiting after income changes
- Ignoring required minimum distribution differences in long-term planning
- Converting to Roth without estimating the current-year tax bill first
