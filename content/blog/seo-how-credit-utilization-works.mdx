Credit utilization is a behavior pattern, not a one-time number. What helps most is controlling **when** balances report and **where** balances concentrate.

## Start with two utilization numbers every month

Track both:

- **overall utilization** across all revolving limits
- **highest single-card utilization**

You can have decent overall utilization while one card reports stress, which can still weaken score momentum.

## Build a 30-day utilization calendar

### Week 1: map close dates and due dates

Create a one-page tracker for each card:

- statement close date
- due date
- current balance
- target reported balance

### Week 2–3: pre-close balance shaping

Before each statement closes:

- push cards above ~35% down toward your target zone
- keep at least one card reporting low activity

### Week 4: concentration reset

Move recurring spend from your highest-utilized card to lower-utilization cards when possible.

## Worked example: same spending, better reporting

Starting profile:

- Card A: $2,500 limit, reports $1,450 (58%)
- Card B: $8,000 limit, reports $900 (11%)
- Card C: $6,000 limit, reports $420 (7%)
- Overall utilization: 17.7%

After two cycles using pre-close payments:

- Card A reports $650 (26%)
- Card B reports $1,050 (13%)
- Card C reports $500 (8%)
- Overall utilization: 11.5%

No new card needed—just reporting control.

## Decision fork: single-card payoff vs spread payments

Use this decision rule:

- If one card is above 50%, target that card first until below ~30%.
- After concentration falls, spread extra payments based on APR and statement timing.

This prevents “good overall score, bad card concentration” problems.

## Mistakes that erase progress

- waiting until due date instead of paying before statement close
- closing old no-fee cards that support total limit
- opening new credit right before mortgage or auto-loan shopping
