Many people focus on one utilization number. Scoring models look at a fuller pattern: how much you use overall, how concentrated balances are, and whether high balances repeat month after month.

## The three-metric dashboard

Track these on a monthly sheet:

1. **Overall utilization** (all revolving balances / all limits)
2. **Highest single-card utilization**
3. **Number of cards reporting balances**

Improvement usually comes from reducing concentration, not from opening multiple new accounts.

## Month-by-month playbook

### Week 1: statement calendar setup
Record statement close dates for every card.

### Week 2: pre-close payment rule
For any card above 30%, pay down before the statement closes.

### Week 3: spend routing rule
Shift recurring spend away from the most-utilized card until balances normalize.

### Week 4: trend review
Compare this month’s three metrics against last month and adjust.

## Example that surprises borrowers

- Card A limit: $1,500, balance: $900 (60%)
- Card B limit: $8,500, balance: $850 (10%)
- Overall utilization: 17.4%

Overall looks acceptable, but Card A concentration can still suppress score momentum.

## Common bad moves

- Closing old no-fee cards that support total available credit.
- Carrying interest due to the myth that debt “helps” score building.
- Applying for multiple cards right before mortgage or auto loan shopping.

## Decision: pay one card hard or spread payments?

If one card is above 50% utilization, concentrated payoff often improves score momentum faster than evenly spreading extra cash across all cards. Once the highest-utilization card drops below ~30%, shift to broader paydown. This “de-concentrate first” approach usually outperforms random balance reductions.

## 45-day improvement sprint

If you are preparing for a loan application, run this short sprint:

- Day 1: identify cards above 40% utilization.
- Day 15: push those cards below 30% before statement close.
- Day 30: push highest card below 20% if possible.
- Day 45: avoid new inquiries and keep balances stable.

The goal is stable reporting, not one-time manipulation.
