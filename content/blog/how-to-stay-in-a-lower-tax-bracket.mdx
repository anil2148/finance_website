Bracket management means intentionally controlling how much income lands in each tax band. Keeping dollars in cheaper brackets can materially lower your tax drag without changing your lifestyle.

## Use pre-tax contributions strategically

Pre-tax contributions to accounts like a traditional 401(k), 403(b), or eligible traditional IRA can reduce current taxable income.

Quick math: a **$5,000 pre-tax contribution** at a **22% marginal rate** saves about **$1,100** in federal tax for that year.

## Time income when you can

Some income timing is flexible:

- bonuses that can be deferred into January,
- self-employment invoices that can shift across year-end,
- stock-comp timing choices when your plan allows.

This is not about aggressive schemes. It is about legal timing choices that help avoid unnecessary bracket spillover.

## Make deductions work for you

Know whether standard or itemized deductions are better in your case.

If you are close to itemizing, bunching deductible expenses into one tax year can sometimes drop enough taxable income to keep more dollars in a lower bracket.

## Build a “bracket buffer”

Your bracket buffer is how much room you have before hitting the next marginal rate.

Small walkthrough: if you estimate you are **$8,000 below** the next bracket threshold, you could:

- convert up to roughly that amount,
- or absorb up to that much bonus,

before spilling into the higher bracket.

## Common mistakes

- Ignoring state taxes while optimizing federal only.
- Over-optimizing one year and missing better multi-year planning.
- Ignoring future low-income years that may offer better conversion or gain windows.

## Next steps

- Estimate your year-end taxable income.
- Identify your current marginal bracket.
- Calculate your bracket buffer.
- Pick one move: pre-tax contribution, income timing, or deduction strategy.
