Lowering DTI is not about paying random balances. Underwriters care most about **required monthly obligations**, so your plan should focus there first.

## Day 0: calculate your true underwriting DTI

Use gross monthly income and only required monthly debt payments.

Formula:

`(housing payment + required debt payments) ÷ gross monthly income`

If your ratio is close to lender thresholds, small payment reductions can have outsized effects on approval outcomes.

## Week 1–2: identify high-impact obligations

Rank debts by required payment, not by balance size:

- high required payment + moderate balance = fastest DTI movement
- refinance candidates that cut monthly requirement without harmful terms
- low minimum-payment debts that barely move underwriting math

This creates a precise payoff/refinance priority list.

## Week 3–6: execute payment reduction moves

Possible tactics:

1. targeted principal payment to lower one large card minimum
2. refinance installment debt only if payment drops materially
3. eliminate small accounts **only** if they carry meaningful minimums

Do not open new credit while this is underway.

## Week 7–12: keep the file underwriter-clean

During this period:

- avoid unexplained large transfers
- keep account balances stable and documentable
- respond quickly to lender requests

A clean file can matter as much as a lower ratio.

## Example: same income, different result

Gross income: $7,800/month.

- Starting obligations: housing $2,250 + other required debt $1,420
- Starting back-end DTI: 47.1%

After targeted payment reduction:

- housing $2,250 + other required debt $980
- New back-end DTI: 41.4%

That shift often moves applications from “borderline” to “approvable with fewer conditions.”

## Decision fork: preserve cash or crush DTI?

Use this logic:

- if ratio is near a lender cutoff, reducing required debt payments usually has priority
- if ratio is already strong but reserves are weak, preserve liquidity to strengthen file stability

You are optimizing for the bottleneck, not following a one-size-fits-all rule.
