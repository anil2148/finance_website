Debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is one of the fastest ways a lender screens affordability risk. A strong credit score helps, but high DTI can still limit approvals or push you into less favorable terms.

## Front-end vs back-end DTI
Lenders often review both:

- **Front-end DTI:** housing payment ÷ gross monthly income
- **Back-end DTI:** total monthly debt obligations ÷ gross monthly income

### Example
Gross monthly income: **$6,500**
- Housing payment: **$1,850** → front-end DTI **28%**
- Total debts (housing + auto + cards + student): **$2,650** → back-end DTI **41%**

A 41% back-end DTI can still qualify in some programs, but your pricing and options may narrow.

## What counts in monthly debt
Usually included: proposed housing payment, auto loans, student loans, minimum credit card payments, personal loans, and other recurring obligations.

Usually excluded: utilities, insurance not escrowed in payment, groceries, subscriptions, and discretionary spending.

## 60–90 day DTI improvement plan
1. **Lower revolving minimums first** by paying down high-utilization cards.
2. **Avoid new installment debt** until after approval.
3. **Refinance only when total cost improves**, not just to reduce payment.
4. **Verify income documents early** to ensure variable pay is counted correctly.

## Tradeoff to understand
Stretching to a longer term can reduce DTI and improve short-term qualification, but often raises total lifetime interest. Compare both the approval benefit and the long-run cost.

## Common mistakes
- Applying before all credit reports are updated.
- Co-signing another loan right before a mortgage application.
- Ignoring fee impacts when consolidating debt.
