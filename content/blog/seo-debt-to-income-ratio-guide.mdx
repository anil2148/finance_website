Debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is one of the first affordability filters in lending. Strong credit alone does not offset a stretched DTI profile.

## Understand both DTI metrics

- **Front-end DTI:** housing payment ÷ gross monthly income
- **Back-end DTI:** all required monthly debt payments ÷ gross monthly income

### Quick example
Gross monthly income: **$6,800**
- Housing payment: **$1,900** → front-end DTI **28%**
- Total debt obligations: **$2,850** → back-end DTI **42%**

Some programs allow this range, but rate options can narrow and underwriting scrutiny often increases.

## What usually counts as debt

Included: housing payment, auto loans, student loans, personal loans, minimum card payments, and similar recurring obligations.

Not usually included: groceries, utilities, subscriptions, and discretionary spending.

## 60- to 90-day DTI improvement plan

1. Pay down revolving balances with highest utilization first.
2. Avoid new installment debt until after approval.
3. Verify income documentation early, especially if variable or self-employed.
4. Review credit reports before application to catch stale or incorrect balances.

## Tradeoff to evaluate carefully

Extending loan term can lower monthly payment (and DTI) but may increase total lifetime interest. Always compare qualification benefit against all-in borrowing cost.
