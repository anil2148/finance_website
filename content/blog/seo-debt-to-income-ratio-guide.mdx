## Who this is for
This guide is for borrowers applying for mortgages, auto loans, or personal loans who want to understand how lenders evaluate affordability.

## Why this matters
Your debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is often one of the strongest approval signals in underwriting. Even with a solid credit score, high DTI can raise your rate or trigger a decline.

## How to calculate DTI
Use this formula:

**DTI = (Total monthly debt payments ÷ Gross monthly income) × 100**

Include:
- Mortgage/rent obligations used in underwriting
- Auto loans
- Student loans
- Credit card minimum payments
- Installment debts and required monthly obligations

Example:
- Monthly debt payments: **$2,400**
- Gross monthly income: **$7,000**
- DTI = 2,400 ÷ 7,000 = **34.3%**

## What lenders typically look for
| DTI range | What it usually means |
| --- | --- |
| Below 36% | Strong profile for many lenders |
| 36%–43% | Often acceptable, but pricing can worsen |
| 43%–50% | Upper range for many conventional programs |
| Above 50% | Fewer approvals and more restrictive terms |

For many conventional mortgage cases, **43% back-end DTI** remains a common threshold. Some programs may allow higher DTI with compensating factors.

## 2026 context
As of March 2026, average personal loan pricing remains elevated for many borrowers. High DTI can push approved applicants toward less favorable APR bands, even when credit score is otherwise decent.

## Key tradeoffs
- Paying debt down before applying can improve DTI and rate offers, but takes time.
- Applying with a co-borrower may reduce DTI if income added exceeds debts added.

## Example scenario
If your DTI is 46%, paying off a $350/month car loan could bring DTI closer to many lender comfort zones. That one change may improve both approval probability and pricing.

## Common mistakes
- Using **net** income instead of **gross** income
- Forgetting card minimums or deferred debt obligations
- Taking a new loan right before a mortgage application
- Assuming DTI alone decides approval
