Most borrowers treat debt-to-income (DTI) like a math problem. Underwriters treat it like a behavior and documentation problem too.

Your approval odds improve when all three are strong:

1. **Required monthly obligations are manageable**
2. **Income documentation is clean and consistent**
3. **Recent account behavior is stable and explainable**

This 90-day plan is built for that reality.

## Step 1 (Days 1-15): Establish your underwriting baseline

Create one worksheet with every recurring debt payment:

- credit cards (minimum payment)
- auto loans
- student loans
- personal loans
- housing obligations (if refinancing)
- co-signed debts that still report to your file

Then add your gross monthly income used for qualification.

### Quick DTI formulas

- **Front-end DTI** = housing payment / gross monthly income
- **Back-end DTI** = total monthly debt obligations / gross monthly income

Most lenders care most about back-end DTI and repayment resilience.

## Step 2 (Days 16-45): Lower the highest-impact obligations first

Do not chase the smallest balances first. Chase the payments that move your qualifying ratio fastest.

Prioritize in this order:

1. Debt with the **largest required monthly payment**
2. Debt that can be refinanced with **material payment relief** and reasonable term cost
3. Revolving balances that are inflating both utilization and minimum payments

### Example: same salary, materially better file

Borrower gross monthly income: **$7,000**

Starting required payments:

- Auto: $620
- Credit cards (minimums): $410
- Student loan: $300
- Personal loan: $270

Total obligations: **$1,600** → Back-end DTI = **22.9%** (before housing).

After 45 days of targeted actions:

- Pay down two cards enough to reduce minimums by $140
- Refinance personal loan to lower payment by $70 (after confirming no hidden fees)
- Eliminate one small installment debt with a $90 payment

New obligations: **$1,300** → Back-end DTI = **18.6%**.

That 4.3-point improvement can move a file from “tight” to “comfortable,” especially when housing costs are added.

## Step 3 (Days 46-75): Protect file stability

This is where avoidable denials happen.

Do this:

- Keep all payments on-time and easy to verify
- Avoid new credit applications
- Avoid unexplained large transfers between accounts
- Keep payroll deposits consistent and traceable

If you do move funds, keep a simple note trail (source, date, purpose).

## Step 4 (Days 76-90): Submission readiness check

Do not apply until these are true:

- Last two months of statements are complete (all pages)
- No missing documentation for deposits or debts
- Updated balances still support your target DTI
- You can sustain this payment profile for the next two billing cycles

## Should you keep cash or pay debt harder?

Use this bottleneck rule:

- If DTI is still near lender cutoffs, reduce required payments first.
- If DTI is already clear but reserves are thin, preserve liquidity and avoid overpaying right before application.

A technically lower DTI is not always better if it leaves you cash-fragile.

## Common mistakes in the final month

- Paying off low-payment debts while ignoring large required obligations
- Opening “just one” new line of credit for convenience
- Letting autopay fail during account transitions
- Sending partial documentation and forcing multiple lender follow-ups
