DTI cleanup works best when you target required monthly obligations, not just balances. Lenders underwrite payment burden, documentation quality, and stability patterns.

## 90-day DTI execution plan

### Days 1–15: freeze new risk

- Do not open new credit lines.
- Pull current statements and confirm reported minimum payments.
- Assemble income and asset documentation in one folder.

### Days 16–45: attack high-impact obligations

- Pay down debts with the largest required monthly minimums first.
- Consider refinance only when payment drops **and** terms remain sustainable.
- Avoid balance transfers that create hidden fees without payment relief.

### Days 46–90: protect file stability

- Keep account activity easy to source.
- Maintain on-time payments and predictable balances.
- Answer lender requests quickly to avoid queue resets.

## Decision: preserve cash reserves or pay debt harder?

Use the bottleneck test:

- If DTI is above lender thresholds, reduce required monthly obligations first.
- If DTI already clears but reserves are thin, keep more liquidity and avoid overpaying debt right before application.

The right move depends on what is currently blocking approval.

## Pre-submission readiness checklist

Submit only when all are true:

- Every large deposit is documented.
- No recent behavior introduces avoidable underwriting questions.
- Your payment profile can stay stable for at least two billing cycles.

## Common borrower errors in the final month

- Paying off the wrong account (small balance, tiny minimum) while ignoring large payment obligations.
- Moving money between multiple accounts without clear notes, creating documentation friction.
- Letting autopay fail during transition periods.

If the goal is approval confidence, optimize for clean documentation and predictable obligations—not dramatic but noisy account activity.
