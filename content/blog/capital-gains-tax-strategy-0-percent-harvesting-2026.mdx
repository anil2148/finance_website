Getting this decision wrong can quietly cost real money: realizing **$30,000** of long-term gains in a 15% year can create about **$4,500** in federal tax that might have been **$0** in a lower-income year. The gap is often bigger than a full year of brokerage fees.

This guide shows how to identify your 0% window in 2026 and use it deliberately, so you can reset cost basis when the math is in your favor instead of reacting at year-end.

If your income is variable—or you’re in a transition year—this is one of the highest-impact tax moves to review before December.

## The 0% long-term capital-gains bracket

Long-term capital gains (investments held more than one year) are taxed on a different schedule than salary income.

In 2026, there is a 0% bracket for long-term gains up to a taxable-income threshold that varies by filing status. If your taxable income plus realized long-term gains stay under that threshold, those gains can be taxed at 0% federally.

You don’t need perfect precision to use this strategy well. You need a practical estimate of where you sit in your [current tax bracket and marginal-rate range](/blog/2026-federal-tax-brackets-marginal-rate-decisions), then how much 0% gains headroom you likely have.

## Real-world scenarios

- **Before:** You sell **$18,000** of long-term gains in a year with little wage income.  
  **After:** Federal tax on those gains is **$0** at the 0% rate, and your cost basis steps up by $18,000.  
  👉 In this situation, harvesting now can reduce taxes on future rebalancing.

- **Before:** You delay selling until a higher-income year and realize the same **$18,000** gain.  
  **After:** At a 15% federal gains rate, tax is roughly **$2,700**.  
  👉 In this situation, timing—not security selection—drove the tax cost.

- **Before:** You need **$12,000** cash and plan to sell one concentrated winner.  
  **After:** You split sales across two years (**$6,000 + $6,000**) and keep both years inside available 0% headroom.  
  👉 In this situation, pacing withdrawals can preserve the 0% treatment.

## When to realize gains

0% harvesting often makes sense in:

- **Sabbatical or gap years** with lower earned income.
- **Early retirement years** before Social Security and required distributions start.
- **Years with unusual income drops** due to job changes or variable business income.

In those windows, you can realize gains intentionally—often by selling and buying back similar market exposure—to reset cost basis while paying 0% federal tax on the gains themselves. ## Timing strategy (with an example)

Imagine:

- You estimate your taxable income is low enough to leave room for **$20,000** of long-term gains in the 0% band this year.
- You realize those gains this year and owe **$0 federal tax** on the gains.
- If you wait for a higher-income year, the same $20,000 might be taxed at 15%, or about **$3,000**.
- If you repeat this for **3 low-income years**, that’s up to **$60,000** of gains repositioned with potentially no federal gains tax.

That’s often the difference between re-positioning a portfolio at no federal gains tax cost now versus paying a meaningful tax bill later.

A simple workflow:

1. Estimate taxable income for the year.
2. Estimate how much 0% gains headroom you have using your [marginal-rate context](/blog/2026-federal-tax-brackets-marginal-rate-decisions).
3. Choose positions you already want to trim or upgrade.
4. Realize gains up to—but not clearly beyond—your target 0% headroom.

## Common mistakes

- **Ignoring state tax:** some states tax gains even when federal is 0%.
- **Forgetting benefit interactions:** an extra **$10,000–$20,000** of gains can reduce ACA subsidies or other income-linked benefits.
- **Realizing too much in one year:** large changes are often better spread over **2–4 years** instead of done all at once.

## Implementation checklist

1. Estimate total income and deductions for the year.
2. Identify how much room you have before gains move from 0% to 15% (for example, plan around a **$5,000 safety buffer**).
3. Pick specific holdings you’d want to change anyway.
4. Execute sales up to your planned realized-gain amount and record new cost basis.
5. Revisit annually in lower-than-usual income years and compare your plan with [lower-bracket tactics](/blog/how-to-stay-in-a-lower-tax-bracket).

## What should you do now?

- If your 2026 income looks temporarily low, harvest gains now up to your estimated 0% headroom.
- If you’re close to the 0%/15% line, avoid aggressive last-minute sales without a buffer.
- If state taxes are high where you live, run the trade anyway only when the federal + state math is still favorable.
- If benefits are income-sensitive, coordinate gain harvesting with subsidy thresholds before placing orders.
- If your portfolio needs a big overhaul, split it across multiple tax years instead of one large realization event.

## FAQ

### Can I sell and buy back the same ETF when harvesting long-term gains?
Usually yes for gains. The wash-sale rule is generally about claiming losses, not gains, but confirm details with your tax pro if multiple accounts are involved.

### How much headroom should I leave below the 0% threshold?
A practical range is a **$3,000 to $5,000** cushion, especially if your year-end income can move due to bonuses, distributions, or contract work.

### Does 0% federal capital-gains tax mean zero total tax?
No. State taxes may still apply, and higher income can affect credits or benefit programs even if federal gains tax is 0%.

### Is this only for retirees?
No. It can work in sabbaticals, career breaks, early business years, or any year with unusually low taxable income.

### What should I check first before placing trades?
Start with taxable-income estimate, then confirm your 0% headroom, then decide which positions you’d want to rebalance even without a tax angle.
