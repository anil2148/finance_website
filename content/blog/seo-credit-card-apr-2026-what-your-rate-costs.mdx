## Who this is for

Anyone carrying a credit card balance month to month, or comparing cards and unsure whether APR differences matter at their balance level.

## Why it matters

The Federal Reserve’s most recent data puts the average commercial bank credit card rate at **20.97% APR** (as of November 2025). On accounts assessed interest (cards where a balance was carried), the average rises to **22.83%** (as of January 2026). Americans are carrying a total of **$1.23 trillion** in credit card debt entering 2026.

What this means in practice: a **$5,000** balance at **22% APR**, paid with minimum payments only, can take roughly **23 years** to eliminate and cost more than **$7,000** in interest.

## How rates break down by credit profile

- **Excellent credit (FICO 740+)**: roughly **17%–21%** ongoing APR
- **Good credit (670–739)**: roughly **21%–24%**
- **Fair credit (580–669)**: roughly **24%–28%**
- **Poor credit**: often **28%+**, with some subprime cards reaching **36%**

Even a 3–5 point APR difference is meaningful once balances are held for multiple months.

## The utilization timing most people miss

Your **statement balance** (not your payment due-date amount) is typically what gets reported to credit bureaus.

That means timing matters:

- Paying before the **statement close date** can lower reported utilization
- Paying only by the **due date** can still leave a high utilization snapshot on your report

Example: on a card with a **$10,000** limit, lowering a balance from **$3,500** to **$800** before statement close drops reported utilization from **35%** to **8%**.

## Key tradeoff: 0% balance transfer fee vs. high APR interest

A 0% balance transfer card usually offers **12–21 months** of no interest, but charges an upfront transfer fee (typically **3%–5%**).

On a **$6,000** transfer with a **4%** fee:

- Upfront cost = **$240**
- Break-even vs. a 22% APR card can occur in under two months
- Every month after break-even during the 0% window is net savings

The catch: after the promo window ends, remaining balance reverts to the standard APR.

## Common mistakes

- Applying for a 0% balance transfer card without a payoff plan before promo expiry
- Making minimum payments while continuing to add new purchases, which can keep balances flat or growing
- Choosing cards mainly by rewards rate without comparing APR ranges

In many cases, cashback cards and balance transfer cards have lower APR ranges than airline-branded travel rewards cards.

## Next steps

1. Find your current APR in your card statement or app.
2. Use a payoff model for three scenarios: minimum-only, fixed monthly payment, and 0% transfer.
3. If your APR is above 24% and your credit score is 670+, compare transfer offers.
