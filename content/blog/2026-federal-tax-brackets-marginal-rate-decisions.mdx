Marginal rate is the tax on your **next dollar** of income. Using it correctly changes decisions on 401(k) contributions, Roth conversions, and timing income.

## 2026 bracket snapshot (single filers)

The 2026 standard deduction is **$15,000** for single filers and **$30,000** for married filing jointly.

| Rate | Taxable income (single) |
| --- | --- |
| 10% | $0 - $11,925 |
| 12% | $11,926 - $48,475 |
| 22% | $48,476 - $103,350 |
| 24% | $103,351 - $197,300 |
| 32% | $197,301 - $250,525 |
| 35% | $250,526 - $626,350 |
| 37% | Over $626,350 |

Crossing into a higher bracket does **not** retroactively tax all prior dollars at that rate.

## Three high-impact decisions

1. **Pre-tax 401(k) increase**
   - In a 22% marginal bracket, adding $6,000 pre-tax may reduce federal tax by about $1,320.
2. **Roth conversion sizing**
   - A $20,000 conversion in a 22% bracket may add about $4,400 federal tax.
3. **Capital gain timing**
   - Spreading gains across years can reduce bracket friction.

## What should you do now?

### If your priority is reducing this year's tax bill

Increase pre-tax retirement contributions first and delay optional taxable events.

### If your priority is long-term tax flexibility

Blend pre-tax and Roth contributions rather than picking one forever.

### If your priority is simple execution

Set one annual tax-planning review in Q4 and avoid month-to-month over-optimization.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Confusing effective rate with marginal rate
- Avoiding raises because of bracket myths
- Ignoring state taxes when evaluating conversions
- Doing large conversions without cash set aside for taxes
