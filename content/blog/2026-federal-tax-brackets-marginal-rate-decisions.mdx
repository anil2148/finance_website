## 2026 federal tax bracket snapshot

For 2026, federal ordinary income still uses seven brackets: **10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, and 37%**.

## Marginal vs effective tax rate

You only need two definitions to make most tax decisions:

- **Marginal tax rate** = tax rate on your next dollar of ordinary income.
- **Effective tax rate** = total federal tax ÷ total income.

If you are making a decision this month, marginal rate usually matters more.

Example: If you are in the 24% bracket, an extra **$1,000** pre-tax 401(k) contribution can lower federal income tax by about **$240**.

:::takeaway
Use your effective rate for awareness. Use your marginal rate for action.
:::

## Mental model: cheap dollars vs expensive dollars

Think of your income as a set of **price zones**:

- Lower brackets = **cheap dollars**
- Middle brackets = **moderate-cost dollars**
- Upper brackets = **expensive dollars**

Every major tax move is the same question in disguise:

1. Should I deduct this dollar now?
2. Should I convert this dollar now?
3. Should I realize this gain now or later?

The winning move is usually the one that keeps more dollars in cheaper zones over time.

:::insight
**Rule of thumb:** Deduct expensive dollars, fill cheap brackets on purpose, and avoid accidental spillover into high-cost brackets.
:::

## Real-world scenarios (with numbers)

### Scenario 1: Single filer in the 22% bracket deciding on an extra 401(k) contribution

- **Situation:** Salary $94,000, single, currently in the 22% marginal bracket.
- **Decision:** Increase pre-tax 401(k) by $6,000 this year.
- **Tax impact:** Rough federal tax reduction of **$1,320** ($6,000 × 22%).
- **Conclusion:** If cash flow allows, this is usually a high-confidence move to reduce taxes now.

**Insight line:** That is a **guaranteed 22% return** on the tax portion, before investment growth even starts.

### Scenario 2: Married couple choosing between pre-tax contributions and a Roth conversion

- **Situation:** Married filing jointly, household income $238,000, near the top of their current target bracket.
- **Decision:** Add $12,000 pre-tax 401(k) vs convert $12,000 from Traditional IRA to Roth.
- **Tax impact:**
  - Pre-tax contribution can cut current federal tax by roughly **$2,880** if in a 24% marginal zone.
  - Roth conversion can create roughly **$2,880** of current federal tax on the same amount.
- **Conclusion:** In high-income years, pre-tax often wins first; use small, bracket-limited Roth conversions only if it fits a longer tax-diversification plan.

**Insight line:** Same $12,000 move, opposite tax direction — one lowers this year’s bill, the other prepays it.

Pressure-test this choice with the [Roth vs Traditional IRA guide](/blog/seo-roth-vs-traditional-ira).

### Scenario 3: Low-income year with long-term capital gains opportunity

- **Situation:** Career break year; taxable income drops to $48,000 (single filer).
- **Decision:** Realize $20,000 of long-term capital gains now or wait.
- **Tax impact:** Depending on total taxable income, some gains may fall in the **0% long-term capital gains zone** federally instead of 15% in a higher-income year.
- **Conclusion:** Low-income years can be ideal for intentional gain harvesting and portfolio cleanup.

**Insight line:** The same gain can cost **0% this year** and **15% later** — timing is the strategy.

Pair this with the [Tax-Efficient Investing Playbook](/blog/tax-efficient-investing-account-location-decisions).

### Scenario 4: Pre-retirement Roth conversion window

- **Situation:** Age 61, recently retired, Social Security not yet claimed, temporarily lower taxable income.
- **Decision:** Convert $40,000 from Traditional IRA this year.
- **Tax impact:** Controlled conversion may fill lower brackets now and reduce future RMD pressure at higher rates.
- **Conclusion:** Convert only up to your planned bracket ceiling and stop when the next dollar becomes expensive.

**Insight line:** This is one of the few windows where you can choose your future tax rate **before** required distributions choose it for you.

## What should you do now?

### Reduce taxes now

If you are already in a moderate-to-high marginal bracket, prioritize pre-tax contributions first (401(k), HSA if eligible, deductible IRA where usable). Each pre-tax dollar is most valuable when your marginal rate is high.

### Build long-term flexibility

Tax diversification matters. Build balances across pre-tax, Roth, and taxable accounts so retirement withdrawals are not forced from a single tax bucket.

### Near the top of a bracket (decision boundary)

If you are close to the next threshold, model moves that keep income below it:

- accelerate deductible contributions
- defer discretionary income where possible
- split large actions across tax years

### Low-income year strategy

In down-income years (job switch, sabbatical, early retirement), consider tactical moves:

- bracket-limited Roth conversions
- long-term gain realization in favorable zones
- one-time rebalancing with lower tax friction

### Roth vs pre-tax decision

Use this quick rule:

- **Higher current marginal rate than expected future rate:** bias **pre-tax**.
- **Lower current marginal rate than expected future rate:** bias **Roth**.
- **Unsure:** blend both and reassess annually.

## Common mistakes that cost real money

1. **Using effective tax rate for tactical decisions**  
   Consequence: You misprice deductions and conversions.
2. **Avoiding raises because of “I’ll lose money in a higher bracket” myths**  
   Consequence: You turn down net-positive income.
3. **Doing large Roth conversions without cash to pay taxes**  
   Consequence: You may sell investments at bad times or underwithhold.
4. **Ignoring state tax when modeling federal moves**  
   Consequence: Your “win” on paper can shrink materially.
5. **Converting too much in one year**  
   Consequence: You jump brackets and overpay relative to a multi-year plan.
6. **Never re-checking strategy after income changes**  
   Consequence: Last year’s good plan becomes this year’s tax drag.

## Action plan: do this before year-end

1. **Identify your current position.** Estimate year-end taxable income and current marginal bracket.
2. **Find your decision boundary.** Calculate how much room is left before the next bracket threshold.
3. **Model one change.** Test one move (extra pre-tax contribution, partial Roth conversion, or gain realization).
4. **Compare outcomes.** Run “do nothing” vs “one change” and estimate tax difference.
5. **Act before key timelines.** Execute before payroll cutoffs, trade settlement windows, and year-end deadlines.

## FAQ

### What are the 2026 federal tax brackets?

For ordinary income, the federal rates are 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, and 37%, applied progressively.

### How do marginal tax brackets work?

Each slice of income is taxed at its bracket rate. Only the dollars in the higher slice get the higher rate.

### What is better right now: Roth or pre-tax?

It depends on your current vs expected future marginal rate. Higher now often favors pre-tax; lower now often favors Roth.

### When is the best time to do a Roth conversion?

Usually in lower-income years, and ideally only up to the top of your chosen bracket.

### Can I realize long-term capital gains at 0%?

In some cases, yes — if taxable income stays within the 0% long-term capital-gains range for your filing status.

### Should I wait until December to make tax moves?

Not always. Earlier planning gives more control, especially for payroll-based contribution changes and multi-trade gain planning.
