Your next-dollar tax rate matters for decisions like pre-tax 401(k) contributions, Roth conversions, and realizing gains. But many people still misuse bracket math.

## Core rule: the U.S. is a marginal tax system

Only dollars within each bracket are taxed at that bracket's rate. Moving into a higher bracket does **not** re-tax all prior income at the higher rate.

## 2026 federal brackets (single filers)

The 2026 standard deduction is **$15,000** for single filers and **$30,000** for married filing jointly.

| Rate | Taxable income (single) |
| --- | --- |
| 10% | $0 - $11,925 |
| 12% | $11,926 - $48,475 |
| 22% | $48,476 - $103,350 |
| 24% | $103,351 - $197,300 |
| 32% | $197,301 - $250,525 |
| 35% | $250,526 - $626,350 |
| 37% | Over $626,350 |

## How this changes real decisions

- A $6,000 pre-tax 401(k) contribution in the 22% bracket saves about **$1,320** in federal taxes.
- A $20,000 Roth conversion in the 22% bracket can create about **$4,400** in federal tax for that year.

Neither choice is always right. The better move depends on current bracket, expected retirement bracket, and time horizon.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Confusing marginal rate with effective rate (effective is typically much lower)
- Declining raises because of bracket myths
- Ignoring deduction timing across years when bunching could reduce two-year effective tax
