A 15-year mortgage usually wins on interest cost. A 30-year mortgage usually wins on monthly flexibility. Your best choice depends on cash-flow resilience, not just math.

## Side-by-side example

Assume:

- Loan amount: $400,000
- 15-year rate: 5.75%
- 30-year rate: 6.25%

Approximate principal + interest:

- 15-year: ~$3,322/month
- 30-year: ~$2,462/month

Monthly difference: about **$860**.

That gap is the core decision variable.

## What the payment gap means in real life

- If your budget can absorb the higher payment consistently, 15-year can slash total interest.
- If income is variable or you need flexibility for childcare, career moves, or investing, 30-year can reduce default risk and preserve optionality.

Many households choose 30-year and make extra principal payments in strong-income months.

## Hybrid strategy: 30-year with 15-year discipline

- Take 30-year loan for payment safety.
- Auto-pay an extra $300 to $800/month when cash flow allows.
- Pause extra payments during emergencies without refinancing.

This approach can capture part of the interest savings while protecting monthly downside risk.

## What should you do now?

### Best option if your priority is lowest lifetime interest

Pick 15-year only if you still keep at least 3–6 months of reserves after closing and your budget remains comfortable under stress.

### Best option if your priority is monthly flexibility

Pick 30-year, then schedule optional extra principal based on quarterly cash flow.

### Best option if your priority is balanced risk and growth

Choose 30-year, allocate part of monthly gap to investing and part to principal reduction.

## Decision stress test

Before committing, test these:

- Could you handle one temporary income disruption?
- Could you still contribute to retirement at your target rate?
- Do you maintain emergency reserves after down payment + closing costs?

If not, the lower payment may be the safer choice.
