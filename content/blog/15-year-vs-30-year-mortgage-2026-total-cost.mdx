Choosing a mortgage term is mostly a cash-flow decision disguised as an interest-rate decision.

## Where rates stand in early 2026

The 30-year fixed average was around 6.65% one year ago. Rates have eased since late 2025 cuts, and spring 2026 has reopened activity for many buyers.

## Example comparison on a $400,000 loan

| Scenario | 30-year fixed | 15-year fixed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monthly payment (P&I) | ~$2,450 | ~$3,270 |
| Total interest paid | ~$481,000 | ~$188,000 |
| Total repayment cost | ~$881,000 | ~$588,000 |
| Payment gap | baseline | ~$820 more/month |

A 15-year term may save about **$293,000** of total interest in this scenario. But the higher required payment must remain comfortable through job and income shocks.

## Decision rule that works in real life

Pick the 30-year if the extra ~$820/month would force reduced retirement contributions, eliminate emergency-fund progress, or leave no room for irregular bills.

Pick the 15-year if the payment is comfortable under stress and you want faster equity build-up and earlier debt freedom.

## Why the 30-year can still be disciplined

A lower required 30-year payment preserves flexibility. You can still prepay principal when cash flow is strong. One extra principal payment each year can materially shorten payoff time without locking in a permanently higher minimum payment.

## Common mistakes

- Choosing a 15-year payment that is tight in perfect conditions
- Choosing a 30-year term with no principal-prepayment plan
- Ignoring tax treatment and itemization assumptions when comparing net costs
