When lenders advertise "low rates," they usually highlight the interest rate, not the complete borrowing cost. For loan shopping, APR is the better comparison point because it reflects required borrowing charges more completely.

## APR vs interest rate: the practical difference

- **Interest rate** is the cost charged on principal.
- **APR** annualizes that borrowing cost and typically incorporates required lender fees.

Two offers can share the same interest rate and still have different APRs if fee structures differ.

## Origination fees change real loan cost

If you borrow $10,000 with a 5% origination fee, you may receive only $9,500 in hand while repaying principal and interest based on the contracted loan amount. That is why fee-aware comparison is essential.

Always collect these fields from each lender before deciding:

1. APR
2. Interest rate
3. Origination fee percentage and dollar amount
4. Loan term
5. Prepayment penalty terms

## The only fair comparison formula

For each offer, calculate:

**Total cost = (monthly payment × number of payments) + upfront required fees**

Then compare total cost and monthly cash-flow fit together. The best loan is the lowest total cost option that remains affordable in a bad month, not just an optimistic month.

## Term length can hide expensive outcomes

Longer terms lower monthly payments, which can feel safer. But extending term often increases total interest materially even at similar APR bands.

Decision rule:

- If two offers are both affordable, prioritize lower total repayment.
- If cash flow is uncertain, choose the shortest term that still clears your stress test.

## Prepayment terms are a silent tie-breaker

Some lenders restrict or penalize early payoff behavior. If your plan includes extra payments, weak prepayment terms can erase expected savings.

Check contract language for:

- principal-only extra payment handling,
- payoff quote accuracy and timing,
- explicit prepayment penalties or processing fees.

## Common errors that create expensive loans

- Comparing one lender's interest rate to another lender's APR
- Accepting the first prequalified offer without competing quotes
- Ignoring fees deducted before disbursement
- Choosing by monthly payment alone without total-cost math
