APR matters, but survivability matters more. The wrong loan is the one that looks fine in a perfect month and breaks your cash flow during a rough one.

## Evaluate offers in this order

1. **Net cash received** after fees
2. **Monthly payment under stress** (simulate 10% income drop)
3. **Total repayment cost** across full term
4. **Prepayment flexibility** and servicing quality

If an offer fails step 2, stop there.

## Example worksheet

Need: $20,000.

| Offer | APR | Term | Origination fee | Net proceeds | Payment profile |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| A | 10.8% | 36 mo | 3.5% | Lower | Easier monthly, longer exposure |
| B | 11.4% | 24 mo | 0% | Full amount | Higher monthly, lower total interest |

Offer B may still be better if payment fits even after stress testing.

## Contract terms that deserve extra attention

Confirm in writing:

- extra payments reduce principal promptly
- no hidden prepayment penalties
- late-fee structure is clearly documented
- autopay setup and posting timeline are reliable

Unclear servicing terms can erase “good APR” benefits.

## Hard rejection rules

Walk away if any are true:

- you need optimistic income assumptions to afford payment
- fee disclosures are incomplete or inconsistent
- lender cannot explain prepayment treatment clearly

A rejected offer now is cheaper than distress later.

## Decision fork: lower payment vs faster exit

Choose lower payment when cash flow is uncertain and reserve is thin.

Choose faster payoff when:

- emergency fund is stable,
- income is predictable,
- and higher payment still passes stress test.

Pick based on stability, not on optimism.

## After signing: first-90-day control plan

Set up autopay immediately, but still verify first two postings manually. Keep a simple amortization tracker so you can see principal movement and test optional prepayments. Early servicing mistakes are easier to correct when discovered in month one rather than month six.
