APR is useful, but it is not the whole decision. Two offers can look close on APR and still produce very different cash outcomes once fees, term length, and payoff flexibility are included.

## The 5 checks that prevent expensive mistakes
1. **Net cash you actually receive** after origination or admin fees.
2. **Total repayment** over full term (principal + interest + fees).
3. **Monthly payment under stress** (for example, temporary 10% income drop).
4. **Prepayment policy** and whether extra payments reduce principal without penalties.
5. **Funding reliability** (how quickly and consistently the lender disburses).

## Example: fee-adjusted comparison
Assume you need $15,000.

- **Offer A:** 10.9% APR, 36 months, 4% origination fee.
- **Offer B:** 11.6% APR, 24 months, no origination fee.

Offer A may look cheaper at first glance, but if fee-adjusted proceeds are lower and term is longer, total borrowing cost can be higher. Offer B might have the better all-in cost if the monthly payment still fits your budget.

## Tradeoffs by borrower profile
- **Cash-flow constrained today:** Longer term can reduce monthly pressure, but increases interest paid over time.
- **Cost-minimizer with stable income:** Shorter term often wins on total cost.
- **Uncertain income (commission/freelance):** Payment flexibility and emergency buffer matter more than small APR differences.

## Red flags
- "Guaranteed approval" messaging before basic underwriting checks.
- Vague fee disclosures or unclear payoff mechanics.
- Pressure to accept quickly before reviewing documents.
