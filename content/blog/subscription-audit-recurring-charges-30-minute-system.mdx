If you’ve ever seen a surprise charge from an old app, streaming service, or “free trial” that you forgot to cancel, you’re not alone. This guide is for anyone who wants a fast reset on monthly spending, and it helps you decide exactly what to keep, pause, or cancel.

By the end, you’ll know where subscriptions hide, how to make the keep/pause/cancel call quickly, and how much you can free up each month.

## Where to look (10-minute scan)

Instead of guessing, run a quick sweep through the places subscriptions usually hide:

- **Bank and credit-card statements (last 90 days):** filter for recurring charges and names you don’t recognize immediately.
- **App stores:** check your Apple App Store and Google Play subscriptions for old apps and auto-renewing trials.
- **Payment services:** review PayPal and other payment apps for automatic or recurring agreements.
- **Email search:** search for “subscription,” “your receipt,” and “auto-renew” to catch what statements miss.

If you don’t recognize a charge, flag it. Don’t decide yet—just build a complete list.

## How to decide what to keep, pause, or cancel

For each recurring charge, ask:

1. **Do I still use this at least once a week or once a month?**
2. **Would I sign up for it again today at this price?**
3. **Does it directly support a priority in my life right now?**

Use simple rules:

- **Keep:** You use it regularly **and** it clearly supports your priorities.
- **Pause:** You use it sometimes, but you wouldn’t miss it for a month or two.
- **Cancel:** You forgot it existed, rarely use it, or wouldn’t choose it again today.

If you wouldn’t re-subscribe today at the same price, that’s usually your signal to pause or cancel.

## Example: what a 30-minute audit can save

Jamie is 32 and assumes subscriptions are “maybe $40–$50 a month.” After a quick audit, Jamie finds:

- A $9.99/month music app that hasn’t been opened in months.
- A $12.99/month video service used for one show.
- A $4.99/month cloud-storage plan no longer needed.
- Two $5.99/month app subscriptions from old free trials.

Total found: about **$40/month** in forgotten or low-value charges.

By canceling or pausing these, Jamie frees up roughly **$480 per year**. Redirecting even half toward savings or investing can compound into meaningful progress over time.

## How often should you do this?

You don’t need to turn this into a hobby.

- Run one full 30-minute setup audit now.
- Then do a quick check once per quarter (or at least twice a year).

Put a recurring reminder on your calendar so this system runs without relying on memory.

## Subscription audit checklist

1. Open the last 90 days of bank and credit-card activity.
2. Highlight recurring charges and names you don’t recognize.
3. Check Apple/Google subscriptions and payment services like PayPal.
4. Make a simple list: service, amount, frequency, and keep/pause/cancel.
5. Cancel or pause at least 1–3 low-value items while already in account settings.
6. Decide where freed-up money goes next (debt, savings, or investing).
