Recurring charges are easy to ignore because they are small, automatic, and often labeled under parent-company names. The result is quiet monthly leakage.

## Why subscription spend drifts

One-time spending is obvious. Recurring spending is hidden by autopay and billing-name ambiguity. Many households carry a double-digit number of active subscriptions, including several unused in the last 90 days.

## The 30-minute audit process

### Step 1: pull 60 days of statements

Download all bank and credit-card statements. Flag any charge that appears at least twice at the same or near-identical amount.

### Step 2: build one list

For each recurring charge, document:

- service name
- monthly amount
- last date used

### Step 3: apply the three-question filter

Cancel when all answers are **no**:

1. Used at least twice in the last 30 days?
2. Would you pay for it again today?
3. Is there no free alternative?

### Step 4: optimize what you keep

For remaining services, compare monthly versus annual pricing and check for family/shared plan discounts.

## Typical findings

- legacy streaming trials
- paused meal-kit plans that resumed billing
- premium app tiers with low active use
- duplicate cloud storage and SaaS tools

Canceling three unused subscriptions at $12-$15/month typically frees $430-$540 annually.

## Common mistakes

- Running one audit and never repeating it
- Canceling shared services without confirming household dependencies
- Choosing "pause" when you intend permanent cancellation
