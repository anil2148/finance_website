## Who this is for

This guide is for beginners who have around $100 to start and want to begin investing without getting overwhelmed by jargon or complex strategies.
If you’ve been putting off investing because you think you need thousands of dollars first, this will help you take a realistic first step.

## What you’ll learn

By the end of this guide, you’ll know:

- Which type of account to open first for long-term investing.
- How to choose a simple, diversified investment instead of stock-picking.
- How to set up small, automatic contributions so you keep going after your first $100.

## Step 1: Choose the right account

Before picking investments, you need an account to hold them.

For most beginners, that usually means:

- A tax-advantaged retirement account (like an employer plan or IRA) if you’re focused on long-term retirement goals.
- A taxable brokerage account if you’re saving for medium- or long-term goals where you may need the money before retirement.

Questions to ask yourself:

- Is this money strictly for retirement, or might I need it sooner?
- Does my employer offer a retirement plan with a match?
- Am I comfortable leaving this money invested for at least 3–5 years?

## Step 2: Start simple with diversified funds

With $100, you don’t need dozens of positions. A single broadly diversified fund or ETF can give you exposure to hundreds or thousands of companies.

Common beginner-friendly options include:

- Broad stock market index funds (tracking a large, diversified index).
- Target-date or “all-in-one” funds that automatically adjust risk over time.

Look for:

- Low ongoing fees (expense ratios).
- A clear, easy-to-understand objective (e.g., “tracks a broad stock market index”).

## Step 3: Set up small, automatic contributions

Your first $100 matters less than the habit you build after that.

Consider:

- Scheduling a small automatic transfer each payday (even $25–$50).
- Treating contributions like a bill you pay yourself first.
- Increasing the amount gradually as your budget allows.

Automation helps you keep investing through normal market ups and downs instead of waiting for “perfect” timing.

## Example: A simple first setup

Imagine:

- You open a tax-advantaged account or brokerage account.
- You invest your first $100 in a low-cost, diversified index fund.
- You set a recurring $50 monthly contribution.

Over time, consistent contributions plus compound growth matter far more than trying to predict short-term market moves.

## Common beginner mistakes to avoid

As you get started, try to avoid:

- Delaying investing for years while waiting to “have more.”
- Putting all your money into a single stock because it feels exciting.
- Constantly checking your balance and reacting to every small price move.

It’s usually more effective to choose a simple plan you can stick with than to chase every new idea.
