Most beginners lose momentum in month four, not year four. The solution is not more market predictions; it is a plan with decisions already written before volatility and life stress show up.

## Quarter-by-quarter operating plan

### Quarter 1 — setup and constraints

- Open only accounts you can explain clearly.
- Set a contribution amount you can maintain for six straight months.
- Keep emergency savings intact before raising portfolio risk.

### Quarter 2 — automate and remove friction

- Turn on recurring contributions tied to payday.
- Simplify holdings if you cannot explain what each one does.
- Track total fees (fund + platform + advisor layers).

### Quarter 3 — volatility protocol

Write your market-drop behavior before you need it:

- At -10%: continue regular contribution.
- At -20%: no allocation changes without written reason.
- At -30%: rebalance only if portfolio drift breaks your rules.

### Quarter 4 — review and reset

- Compare planned vs actual contribution totals.
- Check whether account placement still makes tax sense.
- Set next-year contribution ladder with realistic steps.

## Worked contribution ladder

Example saver starts at $300/month:

- Q1: $300/month
- Q2: $350/month
- Q3: $400/month
- Q4: $450/month

This is less exciting than a “go all in” plan, but consistency usually wins over intensity.

## Three decisions to pre-write now

1. **Raise decision:** when income rises, what percentage goes to investing automatically?
2. **Headlines decision:** what news events are allowed to change your allocation (if any)?
3. **Cash crunch decision:** what minimum contribution keeps the habit alive during tighter months?

## Mistakes to avoid in year one

- Chasing last quarter’s best-performing theme funds.
- Using too many positions to feel “diversified” without understanding overlap.
- Stopping contributions after normal volatility.
