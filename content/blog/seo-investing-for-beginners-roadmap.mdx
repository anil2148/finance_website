## Start with a realistic first-year goal
Most new investors do better with one clear objective: build a repeatable monthly investing habit before trying to optimize every detail.

For most people, that means:
- opening the right account,
- automating contributions,
- choosing a simple diversified allocation,
- and avoiding high-cost behavior mistakes.

## Month 1-2: account setup and cash-flow guardrails
Before investing, check two items:
1. You have a starter emergency buffer (at least one month of essential expenses).
2. High-interest credit card debt is under control.

Then choose account order:
- workplace plan up to employer match,
- IRA (if eligible),
- taxable brokerage for additional flexibility.

If your budget is tight, start with $50-$100 per paycheck. Consistency matters more than starting size.

## Month 3-4: choose a simple allocation
A beginner allocation is often strongest when it is boring and broad. For example:
- 80% diversified stock ETF
- 20% bond ETF

This is not universal advice, but it demonstrates the tradeoff: higher stock exposure can improve long-run return expectations but increases drawdown risk.

## Month 5-6: check fees and platform friction
Two investors earning similar gross returns can end with different net results if one pays higher ongoing costs.

Review:
- fund expense ratios,
- platform or advisory fees,
- trading and transfer limitations.

Even a seemingly small 0.75% annual fee drag compounds over time.

## Month 7-9: automate and stop over-monitoring
Set a recurring contribution schedule and avoid checking balances daily. Frequent monitoring increases the chance of emotional decisions.

A better routine:
- monthly contribution review,
- quarterly allocation review,
- annual full portfolio check.

## Month 10-12: rebalance and upgrade your process
If your allocation drifts materially (for example, after a strong equity rally), rebalance back to your target risk level.

At this stage, improve process quality with:
- a documented investment policy (target allocation, contribution amount, rebalance rule),
- a contribution increase trigger (e.g., +1% after each raise),
- a checklist for major market volatility months.

## Common beginner mistakes that slow progress
- Waiting for a “perfect entry point” and not investing at all.
- Picking multiple overlapping funds that duplicate exposure.
- Switching strategy every few weeks based on headlines.
