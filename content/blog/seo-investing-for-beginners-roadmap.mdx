## Month 1–2: Build your investing base before buying anything
Most investing mistakes happen before the first trade: wrong account type, no emergency buffer, or chasing social-media picks. Start with this order:
- Keep 1 month of essential expenses in cash first.
- Capture 401(k) match if available.
- Then open a Roth IRA or brokerage account based on tax situation.

If your paycheck varies, automate a percentage (for example 8%) instead of a fixed dollar amount.

## Month 3–4: Pick a simple portfolio you can keep for 10+ years
For most first-time investors, a 1–3 fund setup is enough:
- US total market index fund
- International index fund
- Bond fund (optional in your 20s/30s, more important as timeline shortens)

A practical starting mix for a long horizon is 80/20 stocks/bonds. If a 30% drop would make you panic sell, shift to 70/30 or 60/40.

## Month 5–8: Control fees and behavior
Two people can invest the same amount and get different outcomes because of costs and reactions.
- Check expense ratios and platform fees quarterly.
- Avoid daily portfolio checking; use a monthly review schedule.
- Rebalance with rules (for example, when allocation drifts by 5 percentage points).

## Month 9–12: Add strategy without overcomplicating it
Once automation is stable, improve tax efficiency:
- Keep tax-inefficient assets in retirement accounts.
- Use taxable accounts for broad, tax-efficient index ETFs.
- Increase contributions after raises before increasing lifestyle spend.

## Common beginner mistakes by investor type
- **New graduates:** invest too little because they wait for "perfect timing."
- **High earners:** overconcentrate in employer stock.
- **Late starters:** take too much risk trying to "catch up" fast.

### FAQ
**How much should a beginner invest each month?**
Start with an amount you can keep through good and bad markets. Consistency beats sporadic large contributions.

**Should I wait for a market dip?**
Usually no. A rules-based contribution plan reduces timing mistakes.
