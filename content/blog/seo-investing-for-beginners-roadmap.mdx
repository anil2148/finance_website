Most new investors fail for process reasons, not intelligence reasons: they buy before building a cash buffer, pick risk they cannot emotionally hold, or change strategy every time markets drop. This roadmap is designed to prevent those mistakes.

## First choose your investor profile (before choosing funds)
Use this quick profile check:

- **Stability-first investor:** You are new, risk-averse, and likely to panic during big drawdowns. Start with a more conservative mix and smaller automatic contributions.
- **Balanced long-term investor:** You can hold through temporary declines, but still want a clear plan. Use a simple stock/bond allocation and review quarterly.
- **Growth-focused investor:** Long horizon, strong cash flow, and high volatility tolerance. Still set rules to avoid overtrading.

Your profile should determine allocation and contribution pace—not social media opinions.

## Account order of operations (U.S. baseline)
For many households, the practical order is:

1. Build/maintain emergency cash ([Savings Goal Calculator](/calculators/savings-goal-calculator)).
2. Capture employer 401(k) match if available.
3. Evaluate IRA contributions (Roth vs Traditional depends on tax bracket and current/future income).
4. Use taxable brokerage for additional investing.

This order is not universal, but it keeps you from taking market risk before handling basic liquidity risk.

## Your first 12 months, by phase
### Months 1–3: Setup and guardrails
- Open accounts and fund automatically on payday.
- Pick a simple 2–3 fund portfolio.
- Write a one-page “I will not panic sell” rule set.

### Months 4–8: Consistency over optimization
- Increase contribution rate by 1–2% after any raise.
- Rebalance on a schedule, not on headlines.
- Keep a short watchlist of fees, but avoid constant strategy changes.

### Months 9–12: Tax and behavior quality
- Review taxable vs tax-advantaged placement.
- Turn off unnecessary alerts that trigger emotional decisions.
- Predefine what you do in a -10%, -20%, and -30% market.

## Realistic example
Assume you start with **$400/month**, then add **$50 each quarter**. By month 12, your contribution is $550/month with no dramatic lifestyle shock. This “ramp” structure is usually more sustainable than trying to jump straight to an aggressive target.

## Common beginner mistakes to avoid
- Funding a brokerage account while carrying no emergency buffer.
- Chasing “hot” themes instead of staying diversified.
- Confusing activity with progress (more trades ≠ better outcomes).
- Ignoring total fee drag across funds and platforms.
