A CD ladder is a middle path between keeping everything in a variable-rate HYSA and locking all cash into one long CD. You trade a little simplicity for better control.

## Ladder mechanics in one minute

Split one lump sum into multiple CDs with different maturity dates. As each rung matures, either:

- use that cash,
- or roll it into a new longer rung at current rates.

Over time, you create recurring maturity windows so some cash is never far from available.

## Example: 5-rung ladder on $10,000

| Rung | Amount | Term |
|---|---:|---:|
| 1 | $2,000 | 3 months |
| 2 | $2,000 | 6 months |
| 3 | $2,000 | 9 months |
| 4 | $2,000 | 12 months |
| 5 | $2,000 | 18 months |

Once the short rungs mature, roll them forward into the target end-term to establish a steady maturity rhythm.

## When ladders outperform simple HYSA parking

A ladder tends to be useful when:

- you expect savings rates to decline,
- you do not need immediate access to every dollar,
- and you want predictable maturity checkpoints.

If rates rise sharply, HYSAs may look better in the short run. Laddering is primarily a risk-management structure, not a rate-chasing strategy.

## Penalty awareness matters

Early withdrawal penalties can erase yield gains. Before opening any rung, confirm:

- penalty formula (days or months of interest),
- grace period at maturity,
- auto-renew behavior if you do nothing.

## Mistakes to avoid

- Putting your full emergency fund into CDs
- Creating one long CD instead of staggered maturities
- Auto-renewing without checking market rates at maturity
- Ignoring small-bank or credit-union offers that may price better
