A sustainable 401(k) rate is one you can keep during bad months, not just good months.

## 2026 limits and practical interpretation

For 2026, the elective deferral limit is **$23,500**.

- Age 50–59 and 64+: catch-up $7,500 (total $31,000)
- Age 60–63: catch-up $11,250 (total $34,750)

These are upper bounds, not required targets.

## Three-step contribution order

1. Capture full employer match.
2. Stabilize emergency fund and high-interest debt.
3. Escalate 401(k) by +1% each year or raise cycle.

## Practical cash-flow example

Salary: $70,000. A +1% contribution increase equals $700/year gross. In a 22% federal bracket, monthly take-home may drop far less than gross amount suggests, often making the increase manageable.

## What should you do now?

### Beginner path

If you are below the match threshold, raise directly to full match today.

### Low-stress path

Set auto-escalation of +1% every year and review once per quarter.

### Accelerated path

Increase +1% every 6 months until budget strain appears, then hold.

## Common mistakes

- Treating employer match as optional
- Ignoring plan investment selections after enrollment
- Escalating too aggressively and later turning contributions off

### Best option for beginners
For most beginners, start with a simple automatic rule: route **$1062/month** to the goal first, then spend the rest. At a 7% annualized return, **$1062/month** for 10 years can grow to roughly **$183,726** (contribution + growth estimate).

### If your income is $X → do this
- **If take-home income is under $5,900/month:** prioritize stability, keep fixed costs below ~60%, and automate at least **$590/month**.
- **If take-home income is $5,900–$7,700/month:** automate **15%–20%** and add a quarterly increase of **1% of pay**.
- **If take-home income is above $7,700/month:** front-load goals with at least **25% savings/investing** and avoid lifestyle creep.
