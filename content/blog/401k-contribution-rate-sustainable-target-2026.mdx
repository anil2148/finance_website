The right 401(k) rate is not "maximum at all costs." It is the highest percentage you can keep contributing through normal life volatility.

## 2026 contribution limits to anchor your plan

For 2026, the IRS elective deferral limit is **$23,500** (up from $23,000 in 2025).

- Age 50-59 and 64+: catch-up is $7,500 (total $31,000)
- Age 60-63: higher SECURE 2.0 catch-up is $11,250 (total $34,750)

These limits are ceilings. Your first goal is consistency and full match capture.

## Contribution priority order

1. Contribute enough to get the full employer match.
2. Fund IRA contributions if eligible.
3. Return to the 401(k) and raise contributions toward the annual limit.

Stopping at the match is common, but usually expensive over a 20- to 30-year horizon.

## How to find your personal number

Work backward from take-home pay. A 1% pre-tax increase on a $70,000 salary in the 22% federal bracket often reduces monthly take-home by roughly **$47**, not the full gross amount many expect.

A practical method:

- Increase by +1% now.
- Re-test cash flow after one full month.
- Schedule another +1% increase in 12 months.
- Continue until total savings rate reaches your target (often 12-15% including match).

## Traditional vs Roth 401(k) shortcut

- **Traditional 401(k):** stronger current-year tax relief, often useful in 22%+ brackets.
- **Roth 401(k):** no current deduction, but qualified withdrawals are tax-free later.

If your career income is early-stage and likely rising, Roth often deserves a larger share. If your current marginal bracket is already high, traditional contributions can be more compelling.

## Common mistakes

- Freezing at employer match and never escalating as compensation grows
- Taking loans or early withdrawals that permanently interrupt compounding
- Leaving contributions in default cash-like options that are too conservative for long-term retirement investing
